Machu Picchu, Peru

The Inca Trail to Machu Picchu is one of the most famous hikes in the world. It takes you through the heart of the Andes, offering stunning views and a deep dive into the rich history of the Incan civilization.

Machu Picchu, often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas," is one of the most iconic archaeological sites in the world. Located high in the Peruvian Andes, this ancient Incan citadel was built in the 15th century and is a masterpiece of architecture and engineering. Perched at an altitude of around 2,430 meters (7,970 feet), Machu Picchu is not only a symbol of the Incan Empire but also a testament to the ingenuity of ancient civilizations. The site was constructed without the use of wheels, and the massive stones used to build its terraces and structures were precisely cut to fit together without mortar.

The mystery of Machu Picchu’s purpose has intrigued historians and archaeologists for centuries. Some believe it was a royal estate for the Incan emperor Pachacuti, while others suggest it served as a religious or ceremonial site. The citadel features impressive temples, plazas, and agricultural terraces, all designed to harmonize with the surrounding landscape. The Temple of the Sun and the Intihuatana Stone, believed to be a solar clock or calendar, are among the most famous structures that reflect the Incas' astronomical knowledge and deep connection to nature.

Despite being abandoned in the 16th century after the Spanish conquest, Machu Picchu remained hidden from the outside world until its rediscovery by American explorer Hiram Bingham in 1911. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage site and attracts millions of visitors every year. The journey to Machu Picchu, whether by train or hiking the famous Inca Trail, is an unforgettable experience, offering breathtaking views of the Andes and a glimpse into the rich history of one of the world’s greatest civilizations.

Trolltunga, Norway

Located in Norway, Trolltunga is an incredible cliff rock that juts horizontally out from the mountain. The hike is challenging but rewards you with an amazing view that is perfect for a once-in-a-lifetime photo.

The Trolltunga hike, located in the breathtaking fjords of Norway, is a thrilling adventure that draws hikers from around the globe. This iconic trail is named after the dramatic rock formation known as Trolltunga, which translates to "Troll’s Tongue" in English. The hike leads to a stunning rock outcrop that juts horizontally from the mountain, offering a vertiginous view over the deep blue Lake Ringedalsvatnet and the surrounding valleys. The sheer drop beneath the rock creates a dramatic and awe-inspiring perspective, making it a favorite spot for photographers seeking to capture the raw beauty of Norway’s landscape.

The trek to Trolltunga is not for the faint-hearted; it is a challenging 23-kilometer (14.3-mile) round trip that typically takes between 8 to 12 hours to complete, depending on the hiker’s pace and weather conditions. The trail ascends steeply and traverses rugged terrain, including rocky paths, steep inclines, and occasional snowfields, making it essential for hikers to be well-prepared and physically fit. Despite its difficulty, the hike rewards those who undertake it with some of the most spectacular views in the region.

The journey begins at the Skjeggedal parking area, where hikers follow a well-marked path through lush forests and alpine meadows. As the trail climbs higher, the landscape transforms into a dramatic mountain wilderness, with panoramic views of the fjords and valleys below. Reaching Trolltunga itself is a moment of triumph, offering a breathtaking vantage point that feels like standing on the edge of the world. Visitors are advised to plan their hike carefully, ideally during the summer months when the trail is free of snow and the weather conditions are more stable. The Trolltunga hike remains one of Norway's most iconic and rewarding adventures, celebrated for its extraordinary natural beauty and the exhilarating challenge it presents.
